Innovative design

The Stone grinder's uncommon design is one of the main features that makes it one of a kind.

This design is commonly referred to as toothles design. Conventional grinders almost always use prism-shaped teeth which shred the herb inside, but this is not the case with the Stone grinder.

Instead of those teeth, the Stone grinder uses blades or grooves to get the job done.

When inserted, the herb in clumps gets stuck inside those grooves. By turning the top and bottom part against each other, the grooves grind the herb into small pieces. More turns of course mean finer grind, but the final result gets achieved in five to ten turns.

This may seem counter-intuitive on the first glance, but in reality it works even better than the old way. The result you get is a fine, smooth and consistent grid.

Unique mateiral

To make this grinder, I combined traditional materials with something very unique.

The main material used is a dental-grade type IV plaster. It is similar to normal concrete, but it is a much more refined material in every way.

Type IV dental plaster

This type of plaster is normally used by dentists as a step in creating dental prostheses. But, as it turns out, it has may other uses - for example, hobbyists are using it to create realistic miniature stone wall replicas.

I decided to use this material for two main reasons: high strength and nice surface finish.

The strength of the material is really important, because you wouldn't want your grinder to break. The Type IV plaster is known for its hardness as well as strength, making the grinder durable enough for everyday use.

The feel of the material, on the other hand, is just as important.

Stone grinder actually does feel like a stone. It has a smooth, matte finish, and is fairly heavy for its size - it feels similar to the touch as a stone you'd find by the river.

Food-safe Epoxy resin

Even though the plaster really is a quality material, it still leaves a porous surface, which is not too resistant to wear, and can't be cleaned easily. The surface then needs to be properly sealed, which is exactly what I did.

The inside surface of the grinder is coated with a special Epoxy resin, making the grinding features smoother and resistant to wear.

The resin I used is not just any resin. It is certified for food use. Most resin grinders are made of a generic Epoxy resin, but I wanted to go the extra mile making sure the product can be used without any second thought.

Anodized aluminium

Unlike the rest of the grinder, the middle part is made of anodized aluminium.

At first I tried making the grinders completely out of the plaster, but the walls were just too brittle and kept on breaking

So I made the the middle part out of durable aluminium.

The aluminium band is refined through a process called Anodizing. This process makes the surface harder and more resistant to wear. And as a bonus, the process actually encapsulates the pigment into the material instead of just applying it on the top, so the color won't come off.
